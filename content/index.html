1. About

My work centers on two major cases of public knowledge in the 20th century United States, sociology and the Federal air pollution control technocracy. My book on the origins of air pollution control is under advance contract in the Middle Range series with Columbia University Press.

I study how knowledge is made useful, or not. The intellectual history of environmental bureaucrats and the bureaucratic history of sociologists shows that the technocratic and humanist traditions are continuous with each other, for better and worse. I think through this problem through scholarship and direct action.

I am a non-denominational methodologist. My theoretical and methodological work searches for new ways to unify computation, history, and sociology into coherent forms of inquiry. I bring this to my teaching in data analytics, which combines analog and digital computing with discussions on the history and epistemology of statistics.

My students and I at the Queens Data Lab apply findings from my research to help local public organizations make sense of their work. If you are a public organization in Queens NYC with data lying around, get in touch. We can help.
